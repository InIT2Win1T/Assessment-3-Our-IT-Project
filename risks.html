Risks

 

Profitability – There is a possibility that our tool will not make enough money from premium subscriptions to be sustainable. We can offset this with the advertising on our free version but we still need to be prudent in our spending to ensure that our product remains profitable in the future. Our initial costs in creating the product, advertising, listing on relevant stores and assembling our team and equipment will need to be closely monitored so that they do not blow out. Having a unique product that is simple, user friendly and that solves a problem, which we believe we are creating, are some ways to ensure that it will be profitable (10 Tips for Creating a Profitable App, 2021).

 

Market exposure and take up – There is a risk that our product may not receive the exposure that we would like or be as popular as we would like. As our target audience is quite niche the intended audience can be reached quite easily via social media and discussion boards. It would also be beneficial for us to produce short tutorial videos that display the benefits and ease of use to potential users.

 

COVID – In 2020 the AFL season was put on hold and then the season was resumed and shortened. If this was to happen again we could have members that have paid for their subscriptions requesting full or partial refunds. We need to be prepared for this which will mean having a cash reserve available if this was to happen. Also due to lockdowns in capital cities around Australia we need to ensure that we are hiring people that have the capability and work ethic to work from home if required. We will need to conduct a business impact analysis and ensure that we have a business continuity plan in place in the event that we are unable to work at our usual location due to COVID (Business continuity planning for a pandemic | Business Queensland, 2020).

 

App and website development – As most of our group is inexperienced in IT there is a possibility that we may select programming languages or development kits that are not capable of executing the features that we would like to add. To mitigate this it is extremely important that we recruit a programmer or programmers that have knowledge and proven experience with different languages and development kits and that they understand the end goal for our project so that they can recommend the best options.

 

Web scrape – Our process involves scraping AFL Fantasy data from a third party website. This has some risks such as the source being unreliable or removed entirely and also any breach of the website’s terms of service. In terms of being unreliable it would be best if we were able to scrape the date straight from AFL.com.au instead of a third party. Also we will have to do our due diligence and see if we are allowed to scrape the data and if not we will need to find another source that allows us to carry out the web scrape.

 

App stores – App stores may not allow our app onto their stores or may want changes made before being added. This is why we are focusing on the web based version first. Having a web based version also means we can reach all potential users regardless of which operating system they choose to run on their phones. When we are able to list our product on the app store we would ideally launch on the Apple and Android stores around the same time so that we can achieve maximum exposure to potential users. Also, as our web version is coming first it is important to ensure that our web developer has the skillset required to make this website look good and functional on large and small screens.

 

User Interface – There is a risk that we may create a UI that is not user friendly or clunky. It’s important to have this tested by people outside of the project to ensure that our product is intuitive and simple. We could also create a Beta version with early access so that we can get feedback from some users before finalising the product. The same applies to the outputs, in particular any graphs, of our statistical analysis. These need to be easy to read and clearly labelled so that they are easily understood by everyone and it must be easy to manipulate the data, undo a change and have a clear all function. Some things we can do to ensure our UI is user friendly are; do not include elements that do not help the user, use large buttons for critical/frequent functions and include device feedback so users know that they have activated the function  (Babich, 2019).

 

Security – As we will be having a paid service we must protect the data of our customers. We can’t afford to be complacent as customers are critical to the success of our product.) We can mitigate this risk by keeping our security and antivirus software up to date, minimise access to our network, segregate customer data from the rest of our network with a firewall, encrypt customer data and continually test for vulnerabilities among other things. We will also need to develop a data protection policy (Rossi, 2019).

 

Copyright and legal issues – As we are using AFL players and clubs and potentially even the AFL brand we will need legal advice to ensure that we are not using anything that we are not allowed to. If, for example, we are not allowed to use the AFL teams we will need to implement a system that enables the users to understand which team is which without using any trademarks. Initial research has been unclear on whether we can use team names, team colours or player names from the AFL.

 

Hardware – We need to ensure that all of our hardware from laptops to servers are capable of doing the job that we need. Although there are some issues that can’t be mitigated such as our host having issues we need to ensure that what we can control such as the quality of our laptops and network equipment is the best for the job that we can get in our price range. For security purposes it is important that all laptops and routers are relatively new as laptops with conventional BIOS can’t run Secure Boot which in turn means the machine would be vulnerable to malware (Lindros, 2016).

 

Software – We need to ensure that the software we choose is right for the job. We don’t want to be paying for 6 licences to a development kit to find out later that it does not do what we need. A way of mitigating this is mentioned in risk #4 which falls on the shoulders of the app/web developer but we can mitigate this further by purchasing one licence and testing it before committing to further licencing for the whole team.
